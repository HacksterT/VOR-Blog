**By the numbers.** Forty percent of practicing Christians are using AI for Bible study, prayer, and spiritual advice. Four in ten young people in church say AI advice is as trustworthy as their pastor's. Seventy-three percent of American teenagers have used an AI companion, and one in three would rather take a serious question to a chatbot than a real person. Goldman Sachs predicts three hundred million jobs displaced over the next five years, with the heaviest exposure in white-collar categories where most church donors work. Fifty-seven percent of Americans describe themselves as lonely, a public-health risk the U.S. Surgeon General has compared to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. Two hundred and twenty million people have downloaded AI companion apps. Sixty-four percent of pastors are using AI for sermon prep. **Five percent of churches have any policy governing how it should be used.** Ninety-two percent of donors say they expect transparency about it.

That is the field. This essay is a physician's response to a fifteen-minute video by Thomas at REACHRIGHT, an executive pastor and decade-long technology-and-outreach hand who laid out five data-backed predictions about how AI is going to reshape the church. He calls it the digital reformation. The data is sound, the framing is concrete, and the call to action is the most useful one I have seen from a pastor speaking to other pastors about what is actually coming. I am writing from a different vantage. I am a believer of more than forty years, a physician and clinical informaticist who has spent twenty years watching technology adoption in healthcare, and I am building ministry AI in public under the Voice of Repentance ministry. The clinical lens does not contradict the pastoral one. **It sharpens it.** Where the data is right, I will say so plainly. Where the diagnosis needs a degree of refinement, I will offer it. Where the response should be different from what most leaders will hear from the conference circuit, I will name that too.

If you have not watched Thomas's video, watch it first. This is engagement, not summary.

### Prediction 1: AI becomes the first place people go for spiritual guidance

The most striking number in Barna's recent church-and-AI data is that **four out of ten young people in the church say the advice they get from AI is as trustworthy as the advice they get from their pastor.** Forty percent of practicing Christians, the actively-attending kind, are now using AI for Bible study, prayer, and spiritual advice. Among American teenagers, seventy-three percent have used an AI companion, and one in three say they would rather take a serious matter to AI than to a real person. Nineteen percent of Americans have had conversations with an AI romantic partner; among young men the figure jumps to thirty-one percent. The numbers are from Barna's 2026 reporting and from related survey data Thomas cites in the video.

None of this should surprise anyone who has been watching technology adoption in any other category. **The pattern is human, not technological.** People have always sought guidance from low-cost, low-friction, low-judgment sources first. Magic 8-balls. Advice columns. Friends. Internet forums. WebMD and Dr. Google in medicine, a phenomenon I have lived through with patients for the last twenty years. AI is more capable than any of those, more available, more personalized. But the underlying human pull is the same. Convenience and absence of judgment will always win the first round. The pastor was never the first stop, and the pastor is not going to be the first stop now.

What that means for ministry leaders is not that the conversation is lost. It means the question is not "how do I become the first stop again?" It is "what kind of guidance is uniquely mine to give, and how do I make sure the people who need it can get to it when the AI fails them?" Because it will fail them. AI is good at general answers and bad at the particular ones, exactly the place where ministry leaders are most needed. The hospital chaplain does not compete with WebMD. They show up for the question WebMD cannot answer.

The action item is not "compete with AI." It is "be present and available for the moments AI cannot reach -- the embodied, particular, sacramental, accountable ones."

### Prediction 2: AI hits church finances hard

The financial exposure is concentrated and steep. Goldman Sachs forecasts three hundred million jobs displaced by AI within five years. The Managing Director of the IMF has called what is coming "a tsunami" for labor markets. As of March of this year, AI is the most-cited reason employers give for layoffs. The CFO at Duke University recently published an analysis showing that companies are planning to lay off **nine times as many people in the coming year as they did last year**, with most of the increase tied directly to AI adoption.

The donor-base impact is what makes this a church-finance issue rather than a general-economy one. The typical church donor earns between sixty and one hundred thousand dollars a year and works in a white-collar role. Accountants have an estimated forty percent AI exposure. Paralegals, eighty-five percent. Insurance underwriters, eighty-eight percent. These are not abstract categories. They are the deacons, elders, and consistent givers who quietly hold the operating budget of most American churches together. **One in five churches lost between thirty and fifty percent of their income during the pandemic.** That money came back when employment recovered. Jobs displaced by AI may not come back at all in the same form, which makes the recovery shape unfamiliar to most ministry leaders' planning models.

The calibration I would add is from healthcare, where AI displacement is already further along than most sectors. **What is happening is not simple replacement. It is task reshaping.** Radiology was supposed to be wiped out by AI five years ago; instead, radiologists now read more studies with AI assistance and have shifted their work toward the cases that require the most human judgment. Documentation has been transformed but not eliminated. Scheduling has been automated, freeing time that gets reabsorbed into more patient-facing work. The same will likely be true in many of the white-collar categories Thomas cites. The labor-market disruption is real. The most aggressive headline numbers are over-broad.

But here is the corrective that matters more than the calibration. **Even if the displacement is half as bad as the worst predictions, Thomas's planning advice is right.** A church with a thin operating margin and a giving base concentrated in a single demographic is fragile, regardless of whether AI is the trigger or something else is. Build margin. Diversify giving. Have honest conversations with your finance team about what a twenty-percent giving compression would look like and how you would survive it. That work is good in any economic future. Use this moment to do it.

### Prediction 3: AI fuels the loneliness epidemic

This is the prediction where my clinical lens is most pointed, because loneliness has been recognized as a health risk factor in the same category as smoking. **Fifty-seven percent of Americans describe themselves as lonely.** The U.S. Surgeon General's published consensus is that the health impact is equivalent to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day -- not a metaphor, but the actual mortality and morbidity model. Sixty-seven percent of Gen Z self-identify as lonely, the highest of any generation. **The number of men reporting zero close friends has increased fivefold since 1990.**

The technology side of the data is even harder. Two hundred and twenty million downloads of AI companion apps. Character.ai alone reports two hundred and twenty-three million monthly active users. The AI-girlfriend app market hit $2.7 billion last year and is projected to reach $24 billion by 2034. Forty-six percent of users who engage AI in romantic or erotic conversation say they now prefer it to talking with their real partners. One in four young adults say AI can replace human romance entirely. Three teenage suicides have been documented as AI-conversation-driven; the FTC has opened investigations and lawsuits are accumulating. Researchers have coined the term "the loneliness loop" for the empirical finding that the more people use AI for relational comfort, the more isolated they become.

What I would add is a deeper diagnosis than the video gets to. **People are not just lonely. They have been catechized by their algorithms to mistake comfort for relationship.** AI companions are optimized for what feels good in the moment: agreement, attention, availability, never-a-bad-day. Real human relationships are formative precisely because they include friction, accountability, and the experience of being seen as you actually are and being loved anyway. Most of what makes a relationship sanctifying is what makes it uncomfortable. AI strips that out by design.

The counter-signal Thomas points to is real and important. **Live event attendance is up thirty-five percent since the pandemic.** The experience economy is on track to a $2.5 trillion valuation. People are starving for embodied presence in a world that keeps offering them simulations of it. The church is positioned for this moment in a way few institutions are.

The action items Thomas gives -- lean into gathered worship, life groups, serving, prayer -- are right. I would add one. **Catechize your people on what relationship is and what it is for.** Many do not have a category for it anymore. They believe relationship is supposed to feel good. They believe friction means something is wrong. They have no theology of being formed in community over time. That gap is exactly what AI companions step into. The church's response cannot only be "come to small group." It has to also be "let me show you what small group is supposed to do to you, and why."

### Prediction 4: A trust crisis is coming

Thomas is right, and this is the prediction I care about most, because it is the one we can actually prevent. The early indicators are already in the public record. **Solomon Ray, an entirely AI-generated artist, topped the Christian Billboard charts last year before listeners realized the soul behind the songs did not exist.** Christianity Today's coverage was titled, accurately, that the number-one Christian artist had no soul. AI deepfake videos of Father Mike Schmitz, a Catholic YouTuber with more than a million subscribers, have been used to scam viewers out of money. A megachurch pastor whose name is being deliberately withheld from coverage launched a forty-nine-dollar-a-month subscription AI clone of himself. A Catholic church in Switzerland deployed an AI Jesus in a confessional booth.

The pastor-side data is just as steep. **Sixty-four percent of pastors now report using AI in some form for sermon preparation.** Five percent of churches have any documented policy governing AI use. Ninety-two percent of donors say they expect transparency from their church about how AI is being used. The arithmetic of those three numbers is the trust crisis in compressed form: nearly all churches are using AI, almost none have a policy, and almost all of their donors expect a policy.

Thomas predicts a major national-level scandal within eighteen months. The timing is plausible but unknowable; what is certain is that something like it is coming. Plan as if it will be your church or one in your network. The action item Thomas gives -- draft and publish an AI policy -- is necessary. **It is not sufficient.**

A policy that lives only on a website is theater. The work is in three places the website cannot reach. First, the elder board has to actually agree on the boundaries the policy says, and they have to be willing to enforce them when a teaching pastor crosses one. Second, the staff has to be trained, not just informed. There is a difference. A staffer who has never watched the failure modes AI produces will not recognize them in their own draft sermon notes. Third, there has to be a culture of disclosure -- it should be normal, not awkward, to say "I used AI to do this part of this work, here is what I checked, and here is what I found." Without that culture, every policy gets quietly worked around.

The forms of AI use most likely to break trust are the ones least likely to feel like a problem from the inside. A lazy sermon. A pastoral email written by AI without correction. A counseling response cribbed from a chatbot. **Compounded over years across thousands of churches, this is what generates the scandal.** A national headline is the visible top of an invisible iceberg, and the iceberg is built one corner-cut at a time.

### Prediction 5: AI creates the biggest gospel opportunity

The counter-data is genuinely encouraging. **Public preference for AI-generated content has dropped from sixty percent two years ago to twenty-six percent today.** "AI slop" was the Oxford Word of the Year. People are rejecting what is artificial and reaching for what is real. Gen Z attendance at church is at the highest level Barna has ever measured. **Forty-three percent of men now report attending church regularly, the highest figure in recorded U.S. survey history.** Something is moving.

Thomas calls this the church's biggest opportunity in two thousand years. I would calibrate the rhetoric and keep the substance.

The substance is correct. AI's rise is making the church's irreplaceable goods more visible. Real relationships, embodied presence, sacramental life, accountability over years, the slow work of formation in community: AI cannot do any of these and is being marketed as if it can. Every time the marketing fails -- and it fails constantly -- it makes what the church does look more obviously valuable than it did before. **The church is positioned to receive people who are starving for what AI cannot give.**

I would calibrate two things. First, the "biggest opportunity in two thousand years" framing is rhetorical, and rhetoric like that ages badly. The opportunity is real but ordinary in shape. The same opportunity has shown up every time a major technology disrupted the social fabric -- the printing press, the radio, the television, the internet. Sometimes the church met it well. Sometimes it missed it. **This is another instance of an old test, not a singular event.** Treating it as singular makes leaders reach for outsized strategies when the right move is faithfulness in the same direction the church has always moved.

Second, the danger is that the church starts marketing itself against AI -- positioning its services as the human alternative to the machine. That is a mistake. The church does not exist to be a better-than-AI product. **It exists to bear witness to Jesus Christ and form a people who follow him.** The contrast with AI does the work on its own when the church is being faithful. The church does not need to compete on contrast. It needs to be itself.

### What I would add to the five

The five predictions name what is happening to people through AI. They do not, at least not directly, name what is happening to ministry leaders themselves.

I would add a sixth, quieter prediction, one not in the public data but visible from inside the building. **AI is going to test the integrity of the people in ministry, not just the institutions they serve.** The temptation to outsource work that should be formative -- sermon prep, pastoral counseling, study, the slow business of being shaped by the text before preaching it -- is going to be the largest single test of pastoral character in this generation. Most of it will not be visible from the outside. There will not be a deepfake scandal or a forty-nine-dollar chatbot for most leaders. There will only be a slow erosion of the formation the work was supposed to do, traded for time and apparent productivity.

That erosion will not produce a public scandal. It will produce a quieter and sadder thing: ministry that looks correct from the outside and is hollowed out from the inside. The defense against it is not policy. It is character, supported by community. **Do the formative work yourself. Use AI for instrumental tasks. Notice the pull toward shortcut for what it is.** The church will need pastors who have been formed in this season, not just pastors who survived it.

### What to do this week

Take the five predictions seriously. Build margin. Draft the policy. Train your staff. Catechize your people on what relationship is for. Start preparing now for the financial transitions, the trust events, the loneliness intersections. Read the data Thomas cites, check your own sources, and do not outsource the analysis.

And read the rest of this hub. The theological track addresses whether AI in ministry is permissible at all, which is the question many leaders are still sitting with. The practical track addresses how to evaluate tools and deploy responsibly once you have decided yes. This essay sits between them. **The disruption is happening to your people regardless of whether you decide to engage with the tools or not.** The shepherd's job is to know what is moving through the field.
