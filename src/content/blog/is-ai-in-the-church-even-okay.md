Most ministry leaders I talk to are not asking which AI tool to adopt. They are asking whether they should be anywhere near this technology at all. The question on their face when the topic comes up, the one most of them never quite say out loud, is the same one I have heard from patients about new medical technology for twenty years. **"Is that even okay?"**

There is a practical essay alongside this one that addresses tool selection, decision support, hallucination as a safety event, scope of practice, data stewardship, and local deployment. That essay assumes the reader has already decided AI in the church is permissible. This essay does not assume that. This essay is for the leader who is not sure the underlying question has been answered.

I am a believer of more than forty years. I am also a physician and a clinical informaticist who builds AI for ministry. The fact that I do this is not a pre-answer to the question. It is the result of having worked through it. The shape of that working-through is what I want to give you here. Five questions every faithful leader needs to wrestle with before deciding what tools to use: the eschatological, the theological, the question of authority, the question of formation, and the question of discernment. None of them has a glib answer.

The thesis I land on is this. **AI in the church is not categorically forbidden. It is not categorically blessed either. It is a powerful tool, and powerful tools require the same posture every faithful believer brings to any of them: testing, weighing, holding fast to what is good, rejecting what is not.**

If you read what follows and your conviction is "no, not for me, not now," that is a faithful position. The church served Christ for two thousand years without AI. Another decade without it is not a crisis. But if you are going to use AI in ministry, or you are going to lead a church that does, you owe these five questions an honest hearing first.

### 1. On the image of the beast

A real and growing share of believers read AI through the lens of Revelation 13. The image that speaks. The technology that demands worship. The mark of the beast read as biometric or digital. Some of this is genuine prophetic concern. Some of it is internet conspiracy with a thin layer of scripture on top. The line is harder to draw than either side admits.

I want to honor the instinct first. Taking Revelation seriously is a faithful posture. The same Christ who taught his disciples to watch warned that deception would intensify before the end. Skepticism toward technology that is rapidly accumulating power, money, and cultural authority is not paranoia. **A church that has never been suspicious of any new power has not been paying attention.**

But suspicion is not discernment, and discernment is what scripture actually asks of us. Test everything; hold fast to what is good (1 Thess 5:21). Watch (Mark 13:33). Try the spirits (1 John 4:1). None of those commands says reflexively reject. They say weigh.

There is also a humbling pattern from history. Every era of the church has produced believers who identified the technology of their day as the beast. The printing press. The telegraph. Television. The internet. The COVID vaccine. Each time, the prediction was wrong. Some of those technologies turned out to be genuinely corrosive in different ways. None turned out to be the beast. The pattern of misidentification should give us humility about doing it again.

The question to ask of AI is not "could this become the beast?" Almost any technology could be misused that way. The right question is more pedestrian and more useful. **Is the use I am putting it to faithful, transparent, and submitted to Christ's authority?** If yes, the tool is permissible. If no, the tool is not, and the problem is not the tool but the use.

In every AI system I have worked with, I have not seen a coherent claim of religious authority. They are tools. Tools can be misused. They can also be used faithfully. The work is to discern which, and the work is the same kind of work the church has been doing with new technology since the beginning.

### 2. On the work that belongs to the Spirit

The deepest objection is theological. AI mimics things that humans were created to do. Image-bearing intelligence. Relational care. Word-bearing communication. If God made human beings for these things, what are we doing when we build machines that approximate them?

This objection deserves more weight than it usually gets. Genesis 1 makes the human being the bearer of the divine image. Among what bearing the image entails is the capacity for relationship, language, creative work, moral reasoning. AI sits uncomfortably adjacent to several of those capacities. The discomfort is not unfounded.

But there is a precision missing in most versions of this objection, and the precision matters. The image of God is not borne by humans because humans uniquely DO certain tasks. A calculator does arithmetic; that does not threaten human image-bearing because arithmetic is not what makes humans image-bearers. The image is borne in something deeper. **It is borne in the capacity for relational accountability before God. Worship. Repentance. Faith. Love that is freely given. Suffering that has meaning. Death that is not annihilation.** AI cannot do any of these. It is not a person. It does not stand before God. It will not be judged. It will not be redeemed.

That clarity is the line for AI in ministry. AI can support the preacher's study. It cannot do the preaching, because preaching is not text generation; it is a person standing before God on behalf of a people, under the authority of the Word, with the Spirit moving in the room. AI can search scripture. It cannot illuminate scripture, because illumination is the Spirit's work in the heart of a reader. AI can draft a response. It cannot do counseling, because counseling is one image-bearer entering another image-bearer's pain.

Once you see that the Spirit's work and AI's outputs are categorically different kinds of things, the fear that AI replaces the Spirit dissolves. They are not in competition. The real risk is more subtle: lazy or distracted ministry leaders begin to treat AI outputs as if they were the Spirit's leading. That is not an AI problem. It is a discipline problem. We will return to it.

### 3. On the authority to teach

When an AI gives a "biblical answer" to a question, who is teaching? The model? The training data? The vendor? The person asking? The structure the church has historically guarded -- that teaching authority comes through the offices Christ gave to the church, the apostles and prophets and evangelists and pastors and teachers of Ephesians 4:11 -- has no clear place in that exchange. The concern is sharpest in Reformed, confessional, and traditional Christian settings, but the underlying question belongs to every tradition that takes ecclesiology seriously.

The teaching of the Bible is not the production of correct sentences about the Bible. **It is a person, accountable to a community, under the discipline of the Spirit and the historic witness of the church, speaking what they have wrestled with into the lives of those they shepherd.** AI does none of that. AI gives output; it does not stand under correction. It cannot be disciplined. It cannot be removed from office. It is not a member of the church.

That makes AI usable, but not authoritative. Treat it the way a serious student treats a concordance, a commentary, or a colleague's notes. It informs. It does not preach. It does not pronounce. It does not absolve. It does not stand in the place of the elder, the pastor, the catechist, or the teacher whose ordination is recognized by the church.

The danger is not AI usage. It is AI as authority. When a leader passes an AI's output along to the congregation as if it bore the weight of teaching that has been wrestled with, prayed over, and submitted to community accountability, the chain of authority is broken. **The break is not because AI was used. It is because the leader skipped the wrestling.**

The corrective is straightforward. Name AI as what it is. A tool of preparation, not a substitute for office. Be transparent about it. Do not pass off AI-generated material as your own teaching, and do not present it as the church's teaching. It is research. Call it research. Then do the actual work that turns research into preaching.

### 4. On work that is supposed to form you

There is preparation in ministry that is supposed to form the person doing it. The pastor who wrestles with a passage for a week of prayer and study is not just producing a sermon. They are being shaped. The hours of struggle, of being convicted by the text, of bringing their own life under the Word before they bring it to the people: that is formation, not task completion. **Formation cannot be outsourced.**

You do not outsource fasting. You do not outsource prayer. You do not outsource the discipline of submitting your own life to the text before you preach it. AI cannot do those things, because those things are not what AI does. The risk is subtler. AI can do enough of the surrounding work that a leader believes the formation has happened when it has been quietly hollowed out.

A pastor who uses AI to generate sermon outlines may end up preaching a workably correct sermon without ever having been broken by the text themselves. A leader who lets AI summarize their pastoral reading may absorb conclusions without ever having processed them in their own moral imagination. A church planter who uses AI to write everything from prayer guides to small-group questions may build a ministry that is technically sound and spiritually empty. The output looks like ministry. The leader has not been formed by it. Over time, the gap shows.

The principle is to distinguish formative work from instrumental work. Instrumental work is task completion. Drafting an email. Compiling cross-references. Summarizing a long article. Translating a passage. AI is fine for those. Formative work is the work that is supposed to shape you. Wrestling with a passage until it has preached you first. Sitting in prayer with a difficult pastoral situation. Composing the sermon you have to preach because the text has gotten hold of you. AI is not fine for those, and using it for them is not a productivity win. It is a slow theft of the leader's own formation.

The test for any AI use in ministry, framed as a question to ask yourself before reaching for the tool: **does this free me to do the formative work, or does it allow me to skip it?** If it frees me, use it. If it allows me to skip, set it down, and pay attention to the impulse that wanted the shortcut.

### 5. On testing the spirits

The instinct to test new technology spiritually is correct. **First John 4:1 commands believers to test the spirits because not every spirit is from God.** AI tools that generate "biblical" content, "prophetic" outputs, or that purport to speak as Jesus or for God deserve serious testing.

The line is straightforward. AI is not a prophet. It does not receive revelation. It does not speak for God. It does not stand under the authority of the Spirit. Any product or use of AI that frames itself in those terms is making a claim it cannot back. Reject those uses categorically. The market is full of tools that overclaim. They are not all malicious; some are just thoughtless. Either way, do not use them.

But testing the spirits is not panic. It is informed weighing. Practical testing for AI in ministry looks like this.

What does the tool say about itself? Tools that overclaim should be set aside. Tools that present themselves accurately as decision support, drafting aid, research assistant, or scripture search can be evaluated on their own terms.

What does the output say? Test the substance against scripture (Acts 17:11). If an AI tool confidently states things that contradict orthodox doctrine, scripture, or the historic witness of the church, that is a content problem. Do not use that tool, and warn other leaders away from it.

Who is using it, and how? A tool used by a discerning, formed leader as one input among many is in a different category from the same tool used uncritically by someone unprepared to weigh its outputs. The same scalpel cuts safely in a surgeon's hand and dangerously in a child's.

Where is it leading? **Tools that subtly reorient a leader away from prayer, study, the Word, and the community of the church toward the AI as a primary source of guidance are spiritually corrosive even if they never say anything heretical.** Notice the orientation, not just the output.

The simple form of the test, the one to repeat to yourself when the moment comes: does this tool lead me deeper into faithful work, or does it pull me sideways from it? Use the first. Refuse the second.

### Why these five before any practical decision

These five questions are not five reasons to reject AI in ministry. They are five tests that any AI use must pass before it earns a place in the ministry of the church. Most consumer AI uses I see in churches today fail one or more of them, not because AI is intrinsically forbidden, but because the leaders using them have not done this work yet.

The work I am building -- a fine-tuned ministry AI, theologically curated, locally deployed, scoped to ministry contexts under the Voice of Repentance ministry -- is the result of trying to make a tool that can pass all five tests. That is not a sales pitch. **The point is that the tests are achievable.** AI can be used in ministry without violating any of these principles, but only if the work to align the tool, the use, and the leader has been done first.

If your honest conviction after reading this is "no, not for me, not now," receive that as a faithful answer and live in peace with it. The church does not need every leader using AI. It needs every leader walking faithfully with what they have been given, in the office to which they have been called.

If your honest conviction is "yes, but carefully," then the practical essay in this series is the next thing to read. It assumes the question in this essay has already been answered. It is for the leader who has decided to engage and now needs the framework for choosing tools, evaluating vendors, deploying responsibly, and protecting the people they serve.

What is not faithful is to deploy AI in ministry without ever having sat with the questions in this essay. The risk is not that you will use AI. **The risk is that you will use it without thinking, and the thinking is the work that protects the people you serve.**
